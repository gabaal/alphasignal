### What Is a Candlestick?

Every trading chart you've ever seen is made up of **candlesticks**. Each candle represents a specific time period — it could be 1 minute, 1 hour, 1 day, or 1 week. The candle tells you four things:

*   **Open:** Where the price was at the start of the period
*   **Close:** Where the price was at the end
*   **High:** The highest price reached during the period
*   **Low:** The lowest price reached

If the **close is higher than the open**, the candle is green (bullish). If the **close is lower**, it's red (bearish).

### The Body vs. The Wick

The thick part of the candle is the **body** — it shows the range between open and close. The thin lines above and below are called **wicks** (or shadows).

Here's what they tell you:

*   **Long upper wick, small body:** The price went up significantly during the period, but sellers pushed it back down. This shows **rejection** at higher prices.
*   **Long lower wick, small body:** The price dropped hard but buyers stepped in and pushed it back up. This shows **demand** at lower prices.
*   **No wicks, large body:** One side completely dominated the period. Strong conviction.

Pay attention to wicks — they represent **failed attempts**. A long upper wick on a daily candle means that during the day, buyers tried to push the price up and failed. That information is more valuable than most indicators will ever give you.

### Timeframes: The Zoom Problem

Here's something that trips up every beginner: the same asset can look bullish on one timeframe and bearish on another.

BTC on the 15-minute chart might be in a terrifying crash. Zoom out to the daily chart and it's a barely visible dip in an uptrend. Zoom out further to the weekly and you can't even see it.

**Rules of thumb:**

*   **1m–15m:** Noise. Day traders live here, but beginners shouldn't.
*   **1h–4h:** Where most active traders operate. Shows the "conversation" of the day.
*   **1D:** The most important timeframe for any trade lasting more than a few hours.
*   **1W:** The "truth" timeframe. If the weekly trend is up, don't fight it.

Always start with the higher timeframe and zoom in. Never the other way around.

### Support and Resistance: The Market's Memory

Markets have memory. If Bitcoin dropped sharply from $80,000 three times in the past month, traders remember that level. When price approaches $80K again, many will sell — creating a self-fulfilling prophecy.

*   **Resistance:** A price level where selling pressure has historically stopped the price from going higher.
*   **Support:** A price level where buying pressure has historically stopped the price from going lower.

The most important thing to understand: **when support breaks, it often becomes resistance (and vice versa).** A level that held Bitcoin up at $70K for weeks — once it breaks below, that same $70K level will likely act as a ceiling on the way back up.
