Position Sizing with the AlphaSignal Risk Matrix: How Much to Risk Per Trade

Most traders focus on finding the right trade. The Risk Matrix exists to answer the question that comes after: how much of your portfolio should you actually commit to it? This article explains how to use the three risk tools in the terminal to answer that question statistically.

Why position sizing matters more than entry timing

A correct entry with an overlarge position that stops you out on normal volatility is a worse outcome than a slightly late entry with a position sized correctly for the asset's actual range. The terminal gives you the ATR Stop on every signal card for exactly this reason: it is showing you the distance to the statistical invalidation level, which you can use directly to size your position.

The basic formula is: Position Size = (Portfolio Risk Percent) / (Stop Distance as a percent of entry price). If your ATR Stop is 5% away from the current price and you want to risk 1% of your portfolio on this trade, your position should be 20% of the ATR-Stop-adjusted maximum. The terminal does not force you to use this formula but it gives you all the inputs to apply it mechanically.

The Risk Matrix: portfolio-level health check

Navigate to Portfolio & Risk in the sidebar. The Risk Matrix view shows your current portfolio's risk characteristics: Value at Risk (VaR), Beta to BTC, Sortino Ratio, Annualised Volatility, and Sharpe Ratio. These are calculated from your positions as tracked in the My Terminal section.

The Risk Matrix. The Institutional Risk Scorecard on the right gives you VaR, Portfolio Beta, Sortino Ratio, Annualised Volatility, and Sharpe Ratio in one panel. The equity curve on the left shows simulated portfolio performance vs the BTC benchmark over the selected period, with rolling Sharpe overlay.

VaR (Value at Risk) at 95% confidence tells you the maximum expected loss over a one-day horizon in 95% of scenarios. If VaR is 3.2%, it means on a bad day (the worst 5% of days historically), losses could exceed 3.2% of portfolio value. This is the number that tells you whether your current portfolio composition is appropriate for your risk tolerance.

Portfolio Beta above 1.0 means your portfolio amplifies BTC moves. A Beta of 1.4 means a 5% BTC drop is expected to cause a 7% drop in your portfolio. In a Dislocation regime where correlations are elevated, a high Beta portfolio is not diversified. It is just leveraged BTC exposure through different tickers.

Sortino Ratio differs from Sharpe in that it only penalises downside volatility. A Sortino Ratio above 1.5 means you are getting more than 1.5 units of return per unit of downside deviation, which is the kind of asymmetric profile that survives volatility regimes without permanent capital impairment.

The Stress Test Lab: what happens in a crash

The Stress Test Lab (accessible from the Portfolio & Risk hub) runs your current portfolio through historical crash scenarios: 2020 COVID crash, 2022 LUNA collapse, 2022 FTX collapse, and others. For each scenario, it shows projected drawdown, peak-to-trough loss, and recovery time based on your current constituent weights.

The Stress Test Lab. Each historical scenario shows the projected impact on your current portfolio composition. Use this before adding a new position: run the stress test and see whether the drawdown in the worst historical scenario is within your acceptable loss threshold.

The practical use is not to predict the future. It is to understand the worst-case scenario before you are in it. Before adding a large allocation to a high-Beta altcoin, run the stress test and see what happens to the portfolio if the FTX collapse scenario repeats. If the answer is a 65% drawdown, that tells you that the allocation is too large regardless of how attractive the signal looks.

The Portfolio Optimizer: what the ML model recommends

The Portfolio Optimizer applies Monte Carlo simulation and a Markowitz Efficient Frontier analysis to your current asset list, then layers the ML Alpha weighting from the Alpha Score to produce an optimal rebalancing recommendation. The output is a set of suggested allocation percentages alongside a memo explaining the rationale for each weight.

The Portfolio Optimizer. The equity curve compares the ML-weighted allocation against the BTC benchmark over the selected period. The constituent weights panel shows the optimal allocation percentages. The Sharpe Ratio output tells you whether the optimised allocation is expected to deliver better risk-adjusted returns than simply holding BTC.

The optimizer is not a trade recommendation in isolation. It is a portfolio construction tool. It will tell you, given your current watchlist assets and the current Alpha Scores, what the statistically optimal weighting looks like. You can follow it precisely or use it as a constraint. Either way, it forces you to think about the portfolio as a whole rather than a collection of individual bets.

Connecting risk tools to position sizing

The workflow before adding any significant new position is: check the ATR Stop on the signal card to size the individual position, then run the Risk Matrix to see how the addition changes portfolio VaR and Beta, then run the Stress Test to see maximum drawdown in a crash scenario. If all three checks are within your acceptable risk parameters, the position is sized correctly. If any one of them fails, reduce the size until all three pass.

This is not a guarantee against losses. It is a process for ensuring that losses, when they occur, are within the range you can survive and recover from without having to abandon your strategy mid-drawdown.
