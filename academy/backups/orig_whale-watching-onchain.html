Whale Watching and On-Chain Intelligence: What Large Wallets Tell You Before the Market Does

On-chain data is the closest thing crypto trading has to seeing the order book of the entire network. This guide explains the three on-chain views in the terminal and what to look for in each one.

Three views, one question

The Market Analytics hub contains three on-chain intelligence views: Whale Pulse, Chain Velocity, and On-Chain Stats. Each answers a different sub-question of the same core question: where is capital actually moving on the blockchain right now?

Whale Pulse answers: are large wallets buying or selling today? Chain Velocity answers: which L1 networks are accelerating in volume and social attention? On-Chain Stats answers: at the cycle level, is the market overheated, fairly valued, or historically cheap?

Used together, these three views give you conviction signals that the price chart and the funding rate cannot give you, because they are measuring real asset movement rather than speculative positioning.

Whale Pulse: reading the 24-hour net flow

Open Market Analytics in the sidebar and click the Whale Pulse tab. The primary chart is the 24-Hour Net Flow History for BTC. It shows the aggregated on-chain transfer volume of large transactions (defined as transactions above 100 BTC) over the past 24 hours, split into inflows and outflows.

A sustained period of net outflow means large wallets are moving BTC off exchanges or into cold storage. This is historically associated with accumulation: entities that plan to hold do not keep assets on exchanges. A sustained period of net inflow means large wallets are moving BTC onto exchanges, which is historically a precursor to selling pressure.

The Whale Pulse Monitor. Total 24H Net Flow is -16,938 BTC with Institutional Sentiment showing BEARISH. The Whale Conviction Scatter plot on the right shows individual large transactions as bubbles: green for inflow, red for outflow, with bubble size representing transaction size. The cluster of red bubbles in the 100-500 BTC range indicates sustained medium-sized distribution, not panic selling.

The Whale Conviction Scatter plot in the centre panel shows individual large transactions as bubbles on a time-vs-size grid. Green bubbles are inflows, red are outflows. A cluster of very large green bubbles in a short time window (a few hours) indicates a coordinated buy from a single institutional entity or fund. A spread of smaller red bubbles over 12 hours indicates gradual distribution rather than panic selling. These behave differently in terms of price impact.

The Execution Time Topography (Polar Matrix) at the bottom of the view shows which trading sessions see the most large-wallet activity. Spikes in the Asian session (indicated by radials pointing toward the 09:00-13:00 UTC range) versus the US session tell you which geography is currently driving institutional flow. This matters for timing entries: if large wallets are most active during the Asian session and you are trading during the European close, you may be entering after the informed move has already happened.

On-Chain Stats: cycle-level valuation

Navigate to the On-Chain Stats tab. This view contains MVRV Z-Score, Realized Price vs Spot, SOPR, Puell Multiple, and NVT Ratio. These are the metrics institutional analysts use to assess whether BTC is historically overvalued or undervalued at the cycle level.

On-Chain Stats. The MVRV Z-Score of 0.82 is within the historical neutral range (danger above 3.0, capitulation below 0). The Realized Price of $79,002 is significantly above the current Spot Price of $62,502, meaning the average BTC holder is currently sitting at an unrealized loss. This is the kind of information that makes position sizing decisions concrete: the average cost basis is overhead, not underneath.

MVRV Z-Score above 3.0 has historically preceded every major BTC cycle top. Below 0.0, it has historically marked accumulation zones. At 0.82 (as shown in the current screenshot), the market is neither extended nor in capitulation. It is in a neutral zone where neither valuation-based bull case nor bear case applies with high conviction.

The Realized Price panel is particularly actionable. When Spot Price is below Realized Price, it means the average on-chain holder is in an unrealized loss. This suppresses sell pressure from long-term holders (they are less likely to sell at a loss) but increases risk of liquidation cascades if spot drops further. When Spot is above Realized Price, profit-taking pressure increases as holders who bought near the mean begin distributing.

Chain Velocity: where capital is rotating

The Chain Velocity view shows institutional capital rotation across major L1 networks using a Velocity Radar (a spider/radar chart) and a ranked Narrative Leaders leaderboard on the right. The three axes of the radar are Momentum, Vigor, and Liquidity. An L1 with a large cyan polygon on all three axes is receiving capital, social attention, and liquidity simultaneously. A flat profile means the chain is quiet.

Chain Velocity Radar. TON leads the Narrative Leaders leaderboard with a velocity score of 7.5, well ahead of ETH at 5.1. The large cyan polygon in the radar chart shows TON scoring highest on the Momentum axis. Cross-referencing: the top Alpha Score assets in Article 2 included NEAR-USD and TON-USD at ranks 1 and 2. This is exactly that multi-view convergence in action.

The Narrative Leaders leaderboard on the right ranks networks by composite velocity score. The score combines on-chain volume acceleration, developer commit activity, and social mention velocity. When a network appears in both the Chain Velocity top 3 and the Alpha Score top 5, that cross-view convergence is one of the clearest buy setups in the terminal. The signal, the on-chain rotation data, and the network momentum are all pointing at the same asset simultaneously.
