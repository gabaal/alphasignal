How AlphaSignal Works (And Why It's Built Differently)

Most trading terminals give you data. AlphaSignal gives you a decision framework. This guide explains what's actually happening under the hood and how to use it properly from day one.

The problem with most crypto dashboards

Most crypto dashboards are aggregators. They pull in price feeds, social sentiment scores, and technical indicators and display them in a grid. The problem is that a grid of numbers does not tell you what to do. You still have to interpret all of it yourself, under time pressure, while the market is moving.

AlphaSignal is built around a different premise: that a small number of statistical signals, correctly framed, are more useful than a large number of raw data points. The entire terminal is organized around that idea.

What a Z-score actually measures

The primary language of the terminal is the Z-score. It shows up everywhere: on signal cards, in the Alpha Score, in the distribution chart on the Signal Hub, and inside the Backtester results.

A Z-score answers a specific question: how far is this metric from its recent historical average, measured in standard deviations? A Z-score of zero means the asset is behaving exactly as it has on average. A Z-score of 2.0 means the current reading is two standard deviations above average, which happens less than 5% of the time under a normal distribution.

In practice this translates directly. A Z-score of 1.0 on a momentum signal means the asset is moving faster than usual, but not unusually so. A Z-score of 2.5 means something is happening that statistically occurs only a few times per year for that asset. That is when the terminal fires a high-conviction signal.

What Z-score does not tell you is whether a move will continue or reverse. A high Z-score in a trending market often means momentum is intact. The same Z-score in a choppy, range-bound market often means mean reversion is likely. That is exactly what the regime layer is for.

The Strategy Firing Density chart (2) shows how many signals fired over the last 30 days. The Z-Score Distribution (3) shows where the current population of signals sits on the bell curve. The three-tier decision hierarchy

The terminal has a lot of views. When you first open it, the sidebar has over 20 navigation items organized into hubs. This is not meant to be used all at once. There is a specific order to it.

Tier one is macro context. Before you look at any individual signal, you need to know what kind of market you are in. The Market Regime classifier (accessible from Macro Intelligence in the sidebar) runs a Hidden Markov Model against the broad market and outputs one of three states: Risk-On, Compression, or Dislocation. This single label tells you which strategies have historically worked in the current environment and which have not.

Tier two is signal confirmation. Once you know the regime, you look at the Live Signals view. The Z-score distribution chart at the top tells you immediately whether signal activity is clustered in negative or positive territory. If you are in a Risk-On regime and the distribution is skewed positively, that is a coherent environment. If you are in a Dislocation regime and the distribution is heavily negative, shorting or sitting out is statistically supported.

Tier three is execution context. Before entering a position flagged by the signal layer, you check the relevant microstructure data: funding rates to see whether you are swimming with or against the crowd, on-chain exchange inflows to see whether holders are distributing, and your position sizing relative to the ATR-derived stop level shown on each signal card.

Most users skip tier one entirely and go straight to tier two. This is the most common mistake. A strong Z-score in the wrong regime is noise, not signal.

Why there are 80+ views and you only need 5 or 6

The terminal covers a wide range of analytical frameworks because different traders have different workflows and different edges. The Derivatives Hub is essential for someone trading perpetuals. The Chain Velocity view is relevant for someone positioning around cross-chain capital rotation. The Narrative Galaxy is useful for catching early mindshare shifts before they appear in price.

None of that means you need all of it every day. A practical daily workflow uses five or six views in a consistent order: the AI Market Briefing for overnight context, the Market Regime classifier for state identification, ETF Flows for institutional conviction, Live Signals for the current opportunity set, and your own watchlist in My Terminal for execution-level decisions.

The rest of the terminal is there when you need to go deeper on a specific situation. If a whale alert fires and you want to validate it with on-chain data, you go to the On-Chain Stats view. If you want to check whether options dealers are short gamma at a key strike before entering a long, you go to the GEX Profile. These are situational tools, not daily requirements.

The Portfolio Optimizer, under Portfolio & Risk. It simulates an alpha-weighted rebalancing strategy against a BTC benchmark over 30 days. The equity curve here shows the current ML-weighted portfolio tracking below benchmark, with a modeled Sharpe of 1.85. The Institutional Risk Scorecard on the right gives you VaR, Beta, Sortino, and Annualized Volatility in one panel.

Free tier vs Pro: where the line is

The free tier gives you access to the Live Signals view, the Command Center dashboard, price alerts, and the AI briefing. This is enough to understand whether the current environment is interesting and to track a few assets you already follow.

Pro adds the ML-powered layers: Alpha Score rankings, the Backtester, the Portfolio Optimizer, the Regime Classifier, full on-chain analytics, derivatives data, and the live signal alert webhooks. These are the tools that shift the terminal from a monitoring instrument into an active decision-support system.

The clearest dividing line is the regime classifier. Free users can see signals but cannot see what kind of market those signals are firing in. That missing context is what makes the Pro tier worth it for an active trader. Signal quality without regime awareness is like reading a compass without knowing which direction you are trying to go.

What to do right now

Open the terminal and navigate to Macro Intelligence in the sidebar. Find the Market Regime view and note the current state. Then go to Live Signals and look at the Z-Score Distribution chart. Ask yourself: does the signal distribution make sense given the regime? That single check will immediately give you a better mental model of the market than most dashboards can provide.
