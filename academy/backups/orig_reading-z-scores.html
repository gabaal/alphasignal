Reading Z-Scores: The Only Number That Actually Matters

Most users see the Z-score and move on without fully understanding what it is telling them. This article explains the number from first principles and gives concrete decision rules for each threshold.

What Z-score is measuring

A Z-score is the number of standard deviations a current reading sits away from its historical mean. The terminal calculates this across multiple inputs simultaneously: price momentum, volume acceleration, social velocity, and on-chain activity. Each asset gets a composite Z-score that represents how statistically unusual its current behaviour is across all those dimensions at once.

The calculation window matters. AlphaSignal uses a rolling 90-day lookback for the mean and standard deviation. This means a Z-score of 2.0 today says: relative to how this asset has behaved over the past 90 trading days, the current reading is two standard deviations above average. That is unusual. It occurs by chance roughly 5% of the time under a normal distribution.

What each threshold means in practice

Reading the Z-Score Distribution chart

In the Live Signals view, there is a chart on the right side labelled Z-Score Distribution with a Gaussian bell curve overlay. This is not a price chart. It shows the statistical distribution of the current signal population across all assets.

The x-axis runs from approximately -2 to +2. Each bar represents how many signals are currently sitting at that Z-score value. When the bars are clustered in negative territory and the bell curve is shifted left, the majority of signals across the entire universe are bearish deviations. When bars cluster near zero, the market is statistically quiet. When a bar appears far out to the right, a small number of assets are showing extreme positive deviations.

The distribution chart is most useful for reading the overall signal environment before you look at individual assets. If the distribution is heavily negative across 50 assets, a single positive Z-score outlier deserves extra scrutiny before you act on it.

The difference between momentum and volume Z-scores

The composite Z-score on each signal card blends multiple inputs. Two of the most important are momentum and volume. These can diverge in ways that tell you different things.

A high positive momentum Z-score with a flat volume Z-score means price is moving faster than usual but without proportional buying pressure behind it. This is a weak signal. It suggests the move is thin and may not sustain.

A high positive momentum Z-score with a matching high volume Z-score means the price move is confirmed by actual capital flow. This is a stronger signal. Institutions are participating, not just retail.

The signal cards in the terminal show the composite score. To see the breakdown by component, click the signal card to open the Asset Deep Dive panel. The quantitative factor web radar chart inside shows each individual vector including Momentum, Volume, Network Activity, Liquidity, Social Hype, and Dev Commits plotted separately.

Why Z-score alone is not a trade signal

A Z-score tells you something unusual is happening. It does not tell you whether the unusual thing will continue or reverse. That depends on the regime.

In a Risk-On regime, a Z-score of 2.5 on a LONG signal means momentum is elevated in an environment where momentum strategies have historically worked. That is the alignment you want.

In a Dislocation regime, the same Z-score of 2.5 on a LONG signal is happening in an environment characterized by elevated volatility, correlation breakdown, and sharp reversals. The statistical outlier is real. Whether it resolves in your direction is much less certain.

The practical rule: never act on a Z-score above 2.0 without checking the current regime first. If the regime is Compression or Dislocation, treat the Z-score as a warning flag that something is happening, not as a directional trade instruction.

Validating a Z-score signal in the Backtester

The Signal Backtester V2 (under Strategy & Backtester in the sidebar) lets you run walk-forward simulations on any asset using the actual live signal data. Before acting on a high Z-score signal on an unfamiliar asset, run a 90-day backtest on that asset with the current Alpha percentage threshold. The Win Rate, Sharpe, Max Drawdown, and Profit Factor metrics tell you how the system has historically performed on signals of this type for this asset.

The Backtester V2. Set the hold period, date range, EMA trend threshold, and minimum Alpha percentage, then hit RUN. The output gives Win Rate, Total Return, Sharpe, Max Drawdown, and Profit Factor for the signal on that specific asset over the selected period.

The Backtester is not a guarantee of future performance. It tells you whether a signal type has had positive expected value on this asset historically. If the backtest shows a 35% win rate on a particular asset, the current Z-score on that asset should carry less weight in your decision, regardless of how large it is.
