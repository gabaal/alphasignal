Derivatives Decoded: Funding Rates, Options Flow, and Gamma Exposure

The derivatives market tells you what professional participants are paying to hold their positions. This article explains the five sub-views in the Derivatives Intelligence Hub and the specific signals worth acting on in each one.

What the Derivatives Hub shows

Open the Derivatives Hub by clicking "Derivatives" in the sidebar. The hub has five tabs: Funding Heatmap, Options Flow, GEX Profile, Vol Surface, and Max Pain. Each covers a different layer of the derivatives market. Together they answer the question: who is positioned where, how much are they paying to stay there, and where are the mechanical levels that will force price movement regardless of directional bias?

Funding Heatmap: reading crowded positions

The Funding Heatmap is the default view and the most immediately actionable. It shows the current perpetual funding rate for every major asset, displayed as a colour-coded grid. Each cell shows the ticker, the annualised funding rate, and a label: High Positive (Longs Overpaying), High Negative (Shorts Overpaying), or Neutral.

The Funding Rate Heatmap. In the current data: MATIC leads with +0.0100% funding (longs paying at 10.9% annualised), while BTC shows -0.0024% (shorts paying at -2.6% annualised). Most assets are showing Neutral, which is consistent with the Compression/Dislocation regime where directional positioning has reduced. When BTC funding turns sharply positive in a Risk-On regime, that is when mean reversion risk is highest on long positions.

Positive funding means longs are paying shorts to keep their positions open. The higher the positive funding rate, the more crowded the long side is. An annualised rate above 30% means longs are paying significantly just to hold, which creates mechanical pressure toward a flush if price stalls. Negative funding means shorts are paying longs. Highly negative funding across multiple assets simultaneously often precedes a short squeeze rally as shorts close positions.

The most useful setup is cross-referencing the funding rate with the current regime and the Alpha Score. If an asset is in the Alpha Score top 5 with a LONG signal but its funding is above 50% annualised, the long side is already extremely crowded. That does not invalidate the signal, but it means entering at full size into a position that a large portion of the market already holds. The asymmetry is worse than it looks.

Options Flow: what professional options buyers are doing

The Options Flow tab shows live Deribit options transaction data for BTC and ETH. The key metrics are Put/Call ratio, the dominant open interest strikes, and net premium flow direction. When large options premium is flowing into call purchases (not just delta hedges), it indicates institutional directional conviction that is typically informed. When premium is flowing into put purchases on a rising price, it indicates hedging or outright bearish positioning from someone with a view.

The net premium flow chart at the bottom of the Options Flow tab is the most important indicator. Sustained positive net premium flow (more premium going into calls than puts over a rolling window) is a leading indicator for upside price action. It takes capital to buy options, and institutions do not spend significant premium on directional options unless they have a view.

GEX Profile: where dealers are forced to trade

Gamma Exposure (GEX) is the least intuitive of the five tabs but often the most precise for identifying price levels that will act as temporary attractors or repellents. The GEX Profile shows the net gamma exposure of options market makers (dealers) at each strike price.

When dealers have positive gamma at a strike (green bars in the chart), they must sell when price rises above the strike and buy when it falls below, creating a stabilising effect. This is why price often oscillates around high positive gamma strikes rather than trending through them. When dealers have negative gamma (red bars), they must buy as price rises and sell as it falls, amplifying moves and creating trending behaviour.

The practical use is straightforward. If the current BTC price is sitting at a large positive GEX strike, expect range-bound, choppy price action. If price breaks above the largest positive GEX strike and into a negative gamma zone, expect momentum to accelerate because dealers are forced to buy along with the move.

Max Pain and Vol Surface: where options expire and what vol costs

Max Pain is the options strike price at which the total value of all open options (both calls and puts) is minimised at expiration. Options market makers have mechanical incentives to see price at or near Max Pain at expiry because it reduces the payouts they owe. This creates a gravitational pull toward the Max Pain strike as expiration approaches.

The practical rule: during the final 48 to 72 hours before a major Deribit options expiry, price tends to drift toward the Max Pain strike. If current price is well above Max Pain and expiry is approaching, that is a timing-based mean reversion setup. It does not override the regime or the Z-score, but it adds a near-term headwind to momentum longs held through expiry.

The Vol Surface shows the implied volatility smile across strikes and expirations. A steep positive skew (calls more expensive than puts for the same delta) indicates the market is pricing in more upside surprise than downside. An inverted skew (puts more expensive) indicates fear of a sharp downside move. The skew direction often leads price direction by a day or two when large options structures are being built by informed participants.
