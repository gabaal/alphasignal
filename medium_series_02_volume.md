# Stop Guessing — How to Read Volume and Momentum

## Price tells you what happened. Volume tells you if it mattered.

***

In Part 1, we learned to read the naked chart — candlesticks, timeframes, support and resistance. If you did the exercise, you now know where the key levels are on BTC.

But here's the problem: price alone tells you *what* happened. It doesn't tell you *who* made it happen, or whether anyone actually cared.

That's where volume comes in. And it changes everything.

### Volume: The Market's Conviction

Volume measures how many units of an asset were traded during a given period. Every bar on the volume chart corresponds to one candlestick above it.

**The golden rule:** Price moves are only meaningful if they're backed by volume.

Think of it this way:

*   **Price goes up + high volume** = Real demand. Lots of buyers are actively paying higher prices. This move has legs.
*   **Price goes up + low volume** = Nobody cares. A handful of trades moved the price. This move is fragile and likely to reverse.
*   **Price goes down + high volume** = Real selling pressure. Institutions or big players are dumping. Take this seriously.
*   **Price goes down + low volume** = Just gravity. Lack of buying, not active selling. Often just a healthy pullback.

### Volume Profile: The Price Levels That Actually Matter

Regular volume bars tell you *when* trading happened. **Volume Profile** tells you *where*.

Instead of showing volume over time, Volume Profile rotates it 90 degrees and shows you how much volume traded at each **price level**. This creates a horizontal histogram on your chart.

The most important concept here is the **Point of Control (POC)** — the single price level where the most volume traded. This is the price the market considers "fair value" for the current period.

**Why this matters:**

*   Price tends to gravitate back toward the POC (mean reversion)
*   Thin volume areas (gaps in the profile) act like "air pockets" — price moves through them quickly
*   High volume areas act like "magnets" — price tends to consolidate around them

### RSI: The Momentum Thermometer

Now let's add our first indicator. **Relative Strength Index (RSI)** measures how fast and how far price has moved in one direction.

It oscillates between 0 and 100:

*   **Above 70:** The asset has moved up aggressively. It's "overheated" — doesn't mean it will crash, but it's stretched.
*   **Below 30:** The asset has been beaten down. It's "oversold" — doesn't mean it'll bounce, but it's compressed.
*   **Around 50:** Neutral. No strong momentum either way.

**The beginner mistake:** Buying the moment RSI hits 30 ("it's oversold, it MUST bounce!"). No. RSI can stay below 30 for weeks in a strong downtrend. RSI doesn't predict — it measures.

**The right way to use RSI:**

*   Look for **divergences**. If price makes a new low but RSI makes a higher low, that's bullish divergence — selling pressure is weakening even though price is still falling.
*   Use it as a **filter**, not a trigger. "I'll only look for longs when RSI is below 40" is a valid filter. "I'll buy every time RSI hits 30" is a recipe for losses.

### MACD: Momentum Shifts

**Moving Average Convergence Divergence (MACD)** sounds complicated but it's just measuring whether short-term momentum is faster or slower than long-term momentum.

*   When the MACD line crosses above the signal line = momentum is shifting bullish
*   When it crosses below = momentum is shifting bearish
*   The histogram shows the gap between the two lines — getting bigger means momentum is accelerating

**The key insight:** MACD is a **lagging** indicator. By the time it signals a cross, the move has often already started. Use it to confirm, not to enter.

### Putting It Together: A Volume-Confirmed Setup

Here's a simple framework that combines what we've learned so far:

1.  **Check the daily chart.** Is price near support or resistance? (Article 1)
2.  **Check volume.** Is today's volume above or below the 20-day average?
3.  **Check RSI.** Is there a divergence forming? Is momentum stretched?

**High-probability setup:** Price touches a support level you identified + volume spikes (buyers showing up) + RSI shows bullish divergence from oversold = strong candidate for a bounce.

**Low-probability setup:** Price touches support + volume is dead + RSI is flat at 45 = nobody cares about this level anymore. It'll probably break.
